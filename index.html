The Crisis

Understanding Plastic Pollution

Plastic pollution is one of the most pressing environmental challenges of our time. Since mass plastic production began in the 1950s, humanity has generated over 9.2 billion tons of plastic waste — and only a tiny fraction has been recycled or properly disposed of.

Plastics persist in the environment for centuries, breaking down into microscopic fragments called microplastics that infiltrate soil, water, and even the air we breathe. These particles have been detected in human blood, breast milk, and placentas.

The problem is accelerating: global plastic production is projected to triple by 2060 if no action is taken. Meanwhile, marine ecosystems, wildlife, and human health face mounting threats from this synthetic tide.

⚠ Did You Know? A garbage truck’s worth of plastic enters the ocean every single minute. At current rates, there will be more plastic than fish in the oceans by 2050.

Addressing plastic pollution requires action at every level: individual habits, corporate responsibility, and sweeping policy reform. The good news — solutions exist, and communities worldwide are already proving that change is possible.

Know Your Plastic

Types of Plastic & Their Dangers

Not all plastics are equal. Understanding resin identification codes helps consumers make smarter choices and understand recyclability.

①

PET — Polyethylene Terephthalate

Common in water bottles and food containers. Widely recyclable but often ends up in landfills. Contains antimony, a potential carcinogen.

②

HDPE — High‐Density Polyethylene

Used in milk jugs and shampoo bottles. One of the safer, more recyclable plastics — but still contributes to pollution when not disposed properly.

④

LDPE — Low‐Density Polyethylene

Found in plastic bags, wraps, and squeeze bottles. Rarely accepted in curbside recycling and is a major ocean pollutant.

⑤

PP — Polypropylene

Used in yogurt containers and bottle caps. Moderately recyclable, but microplastic shedding poses risks to marine organisms.

⑥

PS — Polystyrene (Styrofoam)

Extremely difficult to recycle and highly persistent. Breaks into tiny beads that are ingested by marine animals at an alarming rate.

③

PVC — Polyvinyl Chloride

Used in pipes, flooring, and packaging. Releases toxic dioxins when manufactured or burned. Considered one of the most environmentally hazardous plastics.

Consequences

Environmental & Health Impacts

🌎 Marine Life Threats

  • Over 1 million seabirds die annually from plastic ingestion or entanglement
  • 100,000+ marine mammals killed by plastic pollution each year
  • Microplastics found in 700+ marine species, including those we eat
  • Coral reefs smothered by plastic debris, reducing photosynthesis by up to 40%
  • Ghost fishing nets trap and kill millions of fish, turtles, and dolphins annually

👤 Human Health Risks

  • Microplastics detected in human blood, lungs, brain tissue, and placentas
  • BPA and phthalates from plastics disrupt hormones and reproductive health
  • Burning plastic releases toxic dioxins linked to cancer and immune disorders
  • Contaminated seafood exposes millions of people to plastic-borne toxins daily
  • Average person consumes ~5 grams of microplastics per week (a credit card’s worth)
